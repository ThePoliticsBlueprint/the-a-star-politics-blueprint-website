The Top 5 Common Mistakes in A-Level Politics & How to Avoid Them

It's easy to get lost in the sheer volume of content in A-Level Politics. While knowing the core theories and concepts is essential, many students trip up on the application and execution of that knowledge in an exam setting. This guide outlines the most frequent mistakes made and provides practical advice on how to avoid them, helping you to move from a good grade to a great one.

1. The Lack of Specific, Substantive Examples

The most common reason for a mid-range mark is the failure to use concrete and detailed examples. While it's easy to remember a general idea, an examiner wants to see that you can apply that idea to real-world political events. Without this, your arguments remain abstract and unsubstantiated.

Instead of simply saying, "Political parties have become less significant," you need to provide evidence. Don't just mention the example; use it to substantiate your argument. Weave your evidence and explanation together to make your argument look more sophisticated. This also makes it easier to write longer paragraphs without unnecessarily dragging out your explanation.

2. Revising Unnecessary Content

This mistake is born out of a desire to be impressive. You might spend hours learning about obscure legislation or minor political figures, which, while interesting, are unlikely to gain you marks in an exam unless they directly support a core point. Examiners are looking for knowledge of the core syllabus, not niche information.

How to Fix It:

  • Stick to the syllabus: Use your exam board's specification as your absolute bible. Every concept you revise should be directly mentioned in the syllabus.
  • Prioritise key events: As mentioned above, it is more beneficial to focus on election cycles that were significant, such as the UK General Election after Brexit where voting was largely based on issues, or the 2000 US Presidential Election (Bush vs Al Gore). For US Politics, this means knowing the most important Supreme Court rulings, key constitutional amendments, and landmark presidential elections. For UK Politics, it means having a solid grasp of key election cycles, recent constitutional changes, and the powers of the Prime Minister and Parliament.

3. Not Having a Set Essay Structure

Many students approach essays without a plan, leading to a muddled and difficult-to-follow argument. A clear structure not only makes your argument more coherent but also helps you to write faster and more efficiently under pressure.

How to Fix It:

For Extract Questions: The strongest structure for these comparative questions is to discuss a similarity between the two extracts in a paragraph, then immediately introduce a key difference within that similarity to counter your initial point. For example, if you are comparing Parliament and Congress, one paragraph could focus on the similarity of them both being bicameral legislatures. You would then counter this by discussing the difference in their relative power: the US Senate and House of Representatives are largely equal in power, whereas the House of Commons is demonstrably more powerful than the House of Lords. Aim for 2-3 of these comparative paragraphs.

For Non-Extract Questions: For a standard essay, base each paragraph on a core theme or idea. A highly effective technique is to lead with the weaker or counter-argument at the beginning of the paragraph (e.g., "While some might argue that the Prime Minister is all-powerful..."). You then introduce a counter-argument (e.g., "...critics would argue that this is limited by..."). Conclude the paragraph by explaining why the second argument is stronger or more significant. This method forces you to demonstrate higher-level evaluation and critical thinking.

For Short 9-Mark Questions (AQA): These questions are designed to test your core knowledge and speed. The key is to get to the point quickly. For each of the three points you make, follow this simple structure:

  • State your point.
  • Provide a piece of evidence.
  • Explain the point in one or two sentences.
  • Explain its significance (or lack thereof) in a single sentence. You should be able to get through all three of these questions in under 45 minutes to give yourself ample time for the longer essays.

4. Failing to Make a Judgement

This is a critical error in an evaluation-based subject. It's not enough to present a balanced argument with points for and against; you must show that you can weigh the evidence and come to a clear, substantiated judgment. The examiner wants to see that you have a developed opinion based on the content you've revised. Many students present all the evidence but then get cold feet and avoid answering the question directly, which is where a lot of marks are lost.

How to Fix It:

  • Don't sit on the fence: Use your introduction to set out a clear line of argument, and use your conclusion to drive home your final, decisive answer.
  • Prioritise your points: Don't just list arguments randomly. Put your strongest paragraphs first and save your weaker paragraphs for later.
  • Use evaluative language: Throughout your essay, use phrases that demonstrate judgment, such as "significantly," "crucially," "fundamentally," or "however." These words guide the examiner to your thinking and show that you're not just listing facts.
  • Always conclude your paragraphs: One of the easiest ways to extend your paragraph is by putting a judgement at the end. This also ensures that you are always showing the examiner that you are critically thinking about your analysis. At the end of each paragraph that is comparing two different arguments, write a judgement statement about which argument is stronger and why (you can talk about recent changes increasing the significance of one argument, for example). If the question is about comparing two extracts with contrasting viewpoints, your judgement should be about which extract is stronger to do with that specific point. Then at the end of your whole essay, write a short conclusion about which side of the argument is stronger overall, or which is more significant in modern day politics.

5. Ignoring the Question and Writing a Pre-Prepared Essay

This is the most dangerous mistake of all. Many students walk into an exam with a few prepared essays and try to shoehorn them into the question asked. Even if your pre-prepared content is excellent, if it doesn't directly answer the question, you will receive a low mark.

How to Fix It:

  • Deconstruct the question: Before you even begin to plan, spend at least a few minutes breaking down the question. What is the core debate? What are the key terms? What is the command word asking you to do?
  • Link, link, link: Every single paragraph, and ideally every sentence, should explicitly link back to the question. Use phrases like "This demonstrates the extent to which..." or "This highlights a key difference in..." to ensure your points are always relevant to the task. At the end of each point and paragraph, always link back to the question. Start each paragraph using the words from the question to make sure you are directly answering it.
